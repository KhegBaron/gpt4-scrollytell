The 90th Percentile That Wasn't.

How a single benchmark score became the defining statistic of the generative-AI boom, and what a careful reanalysis revealed about the authority of numbers in the age of machine intelligence.

Scroll
Act I The Claim

In March 2023, OpenAI released a technical report. One number did most of the work.

Buried in an appendix about evaluations, OpenAI reported that its new model, GPT-4, had scored in the top 10% of test-takers on the Uniform Bar Examination, the standardized test used to license lawyers in thirty-nine U.S. jurisdictions.

Within hours the figure was on the front page of the New York Times, the Wall Street Journal, and Reuters. Within weeks it was cited by Sam Altman in congressional testimony, used to justify multi-billion-dollar AI valuations, and deployed by consulting firms to pitch workforce automation to law-firm partners.

It became shorthand for a very specific claim: that artificial intelligence had reached elite human professional competence.

Act II The Reanalysis

Eric Martínez asked a question OpenAI had not answered.

In March 2024, an MIT researcher published a follow-up paper in Artificial Intelligence and Law. It contained no new model, no secret benchmark. It just asked a single, obvious question: against whom, exactly, was GPT-4 scoring in the 90th percentile?

Against February 2023 test-takers

OpenAI's original comparison pool

01

GPT-4 scored 298 out of 400.

That placed it in the 90th percentile of February 2023 Illinois Bar Exam test-takers, the comparison pool OpenAI used for its headline figure. In a normal distribution of scores centered around 260, a score of 298 looks exceptional.

02

But who takes the February exam?

The February administration is dominated by people who failed the July exam and are retaking it. In February 2023 in Illinois, the pass rate was 62% for first-timers but only 35% for repeat takers , and repeat takers made up the majority of the February pool.

The 90th percentile, in other words, was 90th percentile of a lower-performing population.

03

Against first-time takers, the ranking drops.

When Martínez compared GPT-4's score against first-time test takers of the July exam, a broader, higher-scoring, more representative population — the percentile fell to approximately 62nd. Still above average, but nowhere near the top decile.

04

Against lawyers who actually passed, it drops to the 48th percentile.

If you think the relevant comparison is not "test-takers" but "people capable of practicing law", then you exclude the failures and look only at those who passed. Against this population, the one OpenAI's claim implicitly invoked, GPT-4 is a below-median lawyer.

On the essay portion alone, against the same population: ~15th percentile.

The score was real. The arithmetic was correct. What was wrong was the comparison population , and the comparison population was the entire claim.

Act III The Context

A statistic in motion.

Numbers do not travel alone. To understand how "90th percentile" shaped the public reception of GPT-4 and the AI industry in 2023–2024, follow its career through thirteen months.

  1. MAR 2023

    GPT-4 released.

    OpenAI's technical report claims GPT-4 scored in the top 10% of bar exam takers. Coverage in NYT, WSJ, Reuters, CNN, and the Financial Times within 24 hours.

  2. MAY 2023

    Sam Altman testifies before the Senate.

    Altman invokes GPT-4's exam performance as evidence that AI has reached capabilities requiring urgent regulatory attention, a framing that tends to favor established AI labs over potential competitors.

  3. 2023–24

    The claim becomes common knowledge.

    The "top 10%" figure appears in investor decks, law-school faculty debates, consulting reports on legal automation, and congressional AI hearings, often without a citation at all.

  4. MAR 2024

    Martínez publishes the reanalysis.

    "Re-evaluating GPT-4's bar exam performance" appears in Artificial Intelligence and Law. Shows that against first-time takers GPT-4 is ~62nd percentile; against qualified attorneys, ~48th.

  5. 2024 →

    The original claim continues to circulate.

    OpenAI has not issued a correction. The "90th percentile" figure continues to be cited in media, marketing, and policy. The reanalysis remains a footnote for most readers.

Act IV Use & Beneficiaries

A statistic does work. Who does it work for?

01

Valuation anchoring.

OpenAI closed a tender offer in early 2024 at an ~$86 billion valuation. "Human-level professional capability" was central to that case, and bar-exam performance was the retail-facing evidence.

02

Regulatory framing.

The claim that AI had reached advanced capabilities was used to argue for urgent government oversight, a frame that tends to raise compliance barriers for smaller labs and favor incumbents.

03

Labor-displacement narratives.

Consulting firms, legal-tech vendors, and management commentators cited the figure to pitch workforce restructuring to corporate clients, without examining the underlying methodology.

Act V Why It's Misleading

The number was real. The inference wasn't.

Three category errors let one statistic do enormous rhetorical work.

Comparison Pool
Studied

February Illinois Bar Exam takers, a pool heavily weighted toward people who failed the July exam and are retaking.

↓ generalized to ↓
Claimed

"Test-takers" in general, implying competence comparable to practicing attorneys.

Task Ecology
Studied

A timed, closed-environment exam with multiple-choice questions and short essays, graded against a rubric.

↓ generalized to ↓
Claimed

The cognitive and professional work of practicing law, with all its ambiguity, judgment, and stakes.

Measurement Standard
Studied

A single scaled score on one administration of a standardized test, with no published methodology for the percentile conversion.

↓ generalized to ↓
Claimed

Human-level legal reasoning. Justification for $86B valuations, regulatory frameworks, and workforce displacement forecasts.

The arithmetic was correct. The population, the task, and the inference were not.

Act VI Sociological Frame

Why this is a case about numbers, not just about AI.

Quantification as Authority

Porter (1995)

Trust in Numbers

A percentile rank carries the institutional authority of standardized testing. OpenAI did not need to argue that GPT-4 was intelligent. The number argued for them.

Benchmark as Social Fact

Durkheim (1895)

Rules of Sociological Method

"90th percentile" became an external, coercive belief shaping hiring decisions, policy debates, and capital allocation, regardless of whether anyone actually read the methodology behind it.

Who Designs the Test?

Kasy (2025)

The Means of Prediction

Benchmarks created and publicized by labs with a financial stake in the outcome are not neutral measurements. They are governance instruments.

Conclusion

The most powerful numbers are the ones no one checks.

A single percentile, reported without its comparison pool, published in a technical appendix, repeated without scrutiny, traveled from a model card into congressional testimony, courtroom arguments, and an $86 billion valuation. The reanalysis took one researcher, one afternoon, and one spreadsheet. It has not caught up with the original claim, and in the attention economy of 2023–2024, it never will.
